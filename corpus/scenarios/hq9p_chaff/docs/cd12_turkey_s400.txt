Issue dated: 26 July 2019

Ankara's S-400 gamble: F-35 fallout deepens as first battery declared operational

ANKARA — Turkey has formally activated components of its Russian-supplied S-400 Triumf (NATO: SA-21 Growler) air defense system at the Mürted air base northwest of Ankara, according to officials speaking on condition of anonymity, confirming what open-source satellite imagery had already suggested throughout June. The move follows the delivery of the first S-400 battery elements aboard Russian transport aircraft beginning 12 July, an event Turkish state broadcaster TRT described as a "historic step toward strategic autonomy."

The Pentagon's response was swift. On 17 July the US Department of Defense confirmed Turkey's removal from the F-35 Joint Strike Fighter program, citing the incompatibility of the Russian system with NATO's integrated air defense architecture and, more pointedly, fears that S-400 radar exposure to the F-35's stealth signature could compromise the aircraft's low-observable characteristics. Roughly 100 Turkish pilots and maintenance personnel training in the US on the F-35A were ordered to cease activity and depart by the end of July. Turkey had been slated to receive its first jets later this year and had planned to induct around 100 F-35As over the life of the program — a figure now void.

Under the Countering America's Adversaries Through Sanctions Act (CAATSA), the US Congress has pressed the administration to impose sanctions on Turkey, a NATO ally, for the S-400 purchase — reportedly valued at approximately $2.5 billion, though some estimates put the figure closer to $2.1 billion depending on financing terms and offset arrangements. As of this writing the White House had not announced a formal sanctions package, and analysts in Washington are divided over whether the administration will seek a waiver given Turkey's basing importance at İncirlik.

Turkish officials, including President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, have repeatedly rejected suggestions that the S-400 could instead be swapped for the US Patriot system, noting that earlier US offers came with conditions Ankara considered unacceptable, including technology-transfer restrictions. Defense Minister Hulusi Akar said integration testing of the S-400 with existing Turkish air defense assets — which include US-origin Hawk batteries and the indigenously developed Hisar-O medium-range system — would proceed "on Turkey's own schedule," and suggested the system may eventually be tied into a broader multi-layered network alongside the domestically produced Siper project, still in development.

Questions remain over whether Turkey will seek to operate the S-400 in a manner that avoids linking its radar (believed to be a variant of the 91N6E "Big Bird" acquisition radar associated with the export S-400 configuration) to NATO systems, a step Ankara has floated as a compromise but which Washington and several European capitals have publicly doubted is technically credible. NATO Secretary-General Jens Stoltenberg said the alliance was in "close contact" with Ankara but declined to characterize the S-400 deployment as a breach of alliance obligations per se.

Separately, industry sources note Turkish firm Roketsan continues work on the longer-range Siper interceptor, which Ankara has described in the past as a potential domestic alternative to both the Patriot and the S-400 for future acquisitions, though the program is not expected to reach initial operational capability before the mid-2020s at the earliest.

Turkey's S-400 order reportedly comprises four battalions, with the remaining units expected to arrive in phases through 2020. It remains unclear whether Ankara intends to seek co-production rights for any follow-on order, an arrangement Russian officials have said "remains on the table" but which Rosoboronexport has not confirmed in writing.
