ISLAMABAD, June 2024 — Federal Finance Minister Muhammad Aurangzeb presented Pakistan's defence allocation for FY2024-25 as part of the federal budget tabled in the National Assembly last week, with the Ministry of Defence receiving an estimated PKR 1.804 trillion — a rise of roughly 15% over the revised FY2023-24 figure of approximately PKR 1.57 trillion, according to budget documents circulated to reporters.

Officials from the Finance Division said the increase is largely attributed to inflationary pressure on pay and pensions, fuel costs, and ongoing operational requirements along the western border, rather than any single new acquisition programme. Defence remains the second-largest head of expenditure after debt servicing, accounting for roughly 1.7% of GDP by some estimates — though independent analysts note the figure could be somewhat higher once military pension liabilities, currently booked separately under a civilian pension head, are factored back in.

A breakdown provided to the Senate Standing Committee on Defence reportedly allocates the largest share to the Army, followed by the Air Force and Navy, in proportions broadly consistent with previous years. Committee members were told that a "significant portion" of the increase would go toward salaries and allowances following the government's earlier announcement of a pay raise for armed forces personnel in line with civilian government employees. No detailed service-wise procurement line-items were released publicly, in keeping with longstanding practice.

Committee chairman Senator Mushahid Hussain Sayed was quoted by local media as saying that Pakistan's defence spending "remains modest relative to regional threats," a reference widely understood to mean India's defence budget, which is several times larger in absolute terms. He did not disclose specifics on any ongoing or planned equipment purchases, saying only that "modernization across all three services continues as resources allow."

Separately, the Ministry of Defence Production's budget was listed at approximately PKR 15-16 billion, though the exact figure varies slightly between the pink book and summary tables circulated to press, reflecting what officials described as routine adjustments before final passage. Defence production officials said funding would support continued indigenization efforts at state-run entities including Pakistan Ordnance Factories, Heavy Industries Taxila, and Pakistan Aeronautical Complex, without specifying particular platforms or contracts.

Analysts contacted for comment cautioned that headline defence budget figures in Pakistan typically understate real military expenditure, since certain procurement — particularly big-ticket foreign acquisitions — is believed to be financed through supplementary grants, foreign credit lines, or off-budget arrangements with allied states, and therefore may not be fully visible in the publicly tabled figures. One Islamabad-based defence economist, speaking on condition of anonymity, said the true figure "could be 20-30% higher" once such financing is included, though this estimate could not be independently verified.

The budget document also referenced continued "modernization of the armed forces in line with evolving regional security dynamics," language that has appeared in similar form in prior years' submissions. No further elaboration was provided on specific systems, platforms, or foreign suppliers under consideration for FY2024-25.

The National Assembly is expected to pass the finance bill, including the defence allocation, before the start of the new fiscal year on July 1.
