China's HQ-22 Air Defence System Cements Balkan Foothold as Serbia Confirms Second Battery Operational

Defense Trade Review — China Military News
November 2022 issue

Serbia's HQ-22 (FK-3 export designation) surface-to-air missile batteries — delivered in three tranches beginning April 2020 — have reportedly reached full operational status with the Serbian Air Force and Air Defence, according to regional defence outlets citing unnamed officials in Belgrade. The acquisition, valued at an estimated $800 million–$1 billion (figures vary considerably across sources and have never been officially confirmed by either Belgrade or Beijing), made Serbia the first European operator of a Chinese long-range SAM system and, by most accounts, the first NATO-adjacent state to field one on the continent.

The FK-3, marketed abroad under the HQ-22 designation domestically, is a medium-to-long-range system developed by China Aerospace Science and Industry Corporation (CASIC) — the same state conglomerate behind the HQ-9 family — but the FK-3/HQ-22 is understood to derive from a distinct design lineage, believed by several analysts to share more architectural DNA with the HQ-12/KS-1 program than with the HQ-9. Serbian officers have described the system in local press as a "medium-range" complement to the older Pantsir-S1 (Pancir-S1) short-range guns/missiles already in service, rather than a long-range strategic asset.

Serbia's contract reportedly covers six firing units, organized into what local reporting has called "battalions" or "divizions," each fielding a mix of transporter-erector-launchers (TELs), engagement radars, and command posts mounted on FAP or similar 8x8 chassis. Missile reload times and total inventory numbers remain unclear; one Serbian military commentator, writing under a pseudonym on a regional forum, put the missile stock "somewhere north of 200 rounds," a figure this publication cannot independently verify.

Range figures for the HQ-22/FK-3 continue to be reported inconsistently. Chinese marketing material at recent airshows has cited approximately 100 km against aerodynamic targets, while some Western open-source estimates push this to 120–170 km depending on target profile and altitude. Serbian Defence Minister Miloš Vučević (then deputy minister) stated in an earlier 2020 briefing that the system provides "coverage the country has never had before," without offering further technical specifics.

The delivery drew a formal protest from the United States embassy in Belgrade in 2020, and has periodically resurfaced as a point of friction given Serbia's stated ambitions toward eventual EU integration alongside its deepening defence-industrial ties with Beijing. A second protest, more muted in tone, reportedly followed in mid-2022 after Serbian state broadcaster RTS aired footage of a live-fire exercise at the Pasuljanske Livade range, showing what analysts identified — tentatively — as an HQ-22 TEL alongside older HQ-2 (an earlier-generation, first-generation Chinese SAM unrelated to the HQ-9 line) launch rails still nominally in inventory.

Regional defence commentators have periodically speculated about further Chinese SAM sales in the Balkans, though no other state in the region has confirmed similar acquisitions as of this writing. Some Serbian defence bloggers have also floated — without sourcing — the possibility of Belgrade eventually seeking a longer-range Chinese system such as the HQ-9 family to complement the HQ-22 batteries, though nothing resembling a formal request has surfaced in trade publications or export-licensing chatter monitored by this desk.

CASIC representatives did not respond to a request for comment. The Serbian Ministry of Defence likewise did not respond by publication time.
