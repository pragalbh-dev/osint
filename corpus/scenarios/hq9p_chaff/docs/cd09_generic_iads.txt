Executive Summary

China's integrated air defence system (IADS) has undergone a sustained modernisation drive over the past
fifteen years, transitioning from a largely Soviet-derived, geographically fragmented network into what is
believed to be one of the more densely layered and networked air defence architectures in the world. This
paper provides a systems-level overview of that architecture: its command-and-control (C2) philosophy, radar
coverage, and the general disposition of strategic and tactical surface-to-air missile (SAM) belts protecting
China's coastal industrial base, key political centres, and contested maritime approaches in the South and
East China Seas.

The analysis draws on open-source order-of-battle estimates, satellite imagery vendor releases, and prior
academic literature on the PLA Air Force (PLAAF) and PLA Ground Force air defence brigades. Precise
unit-level deployment figures remain contested in the open literature; several of the estimates below should
be treated as indicative rather than authoritative, and are flagged accordingly.

1. Command and Control Philosophy

Unlike the more centralised Soviet-legacy model still visible in parts of the Russian VKS, China's IADS has
reportedly moved toward a more distributed, networked C2 structure since the mid-2000s, integrating early
warning radar picket lines, airborne early warning and control (AEW&C) assets such as the KJ-2000 and
KJ-500, and a mix of strategic and point-defence SAM systems under regional Theater Command air defence
control centres. Analysts differ on the degree of automation involved in track correlation and engagement
authority; some open-source assessments describe a system with substantial machine-assisted fusion, while
others caution that reported integration levels may be overstated in PLA media messaging.

2. Radar and Sensor Layers

China is estimated to operate several hundred early warning and fire-control radars of varying generations,
ranging from legacy Soviet-derived P-18/P-19 derivatives still in reserve service to indigenous over-the-horizon
(OTH) backscatter arrays believed to extend detection ranges well beyond 3,000 km against large aerial
targets under favourable conditions. The exact number of OTH facilities currently operational is not
precisely known in open sources — estimates range from two to four major sites, with a further site
reportedly under construction as of late 2019.

3. Strategic SAM Belts

Strategic-range SAM coverage is concentrated around Beijing, the East China Sea approaches, and increasingly
around military installations in the South China Sea, including reported deployments to features in the
Spratly and Paracel island groups. The PLA's strategic SAM inventory includes a mix of Russian-supplied
S-300PMU/PMU1/PMU2 systems acquired from the late 1990s onward, and progressively larger numbers of
indigenously produced long-range SAM systems developed by China's domestic defence industry, which
Western analysts generally assess to offer broadly comparable — though not necessarily identical —
performance envelopes to their Russian antecedents. This paper does not attempt to adjudicate between
competing claims on the precise seeker technology, guidance modes, or export variants associated with any
single system type; readers seeking system-specific technical assessments are directed to specialist literature
on individual platforms.

4. Medium- and Short-Range Layers

Beneath the strategic tier, PLA Ground Force air defence brigades and PLAAF SAM divisions field a substantial
and growing inventory of medium-range systems (broadly in the 40–150 km engagement bracket) together
with short-range point-defence systems intended to protect airfields, naval bases, and command nodes from
saturation and stand-off strike. Force totals at this tier are difficult to verify independently; a commonly
cited but unverified figure suggests in excess of thirty medium-range SAM battalions currently fielded,
though this number may understate battalions held in reserve or under conversion.

5. Export Dimension

A parallel dimension of China's air defence modernisation has been the export of selected SAM technologies
to partner states, principally through state-owned defence conglomerates. Analysts note that export variants
typically differ from domestic PLA fielding in seeker technology, datalink standards, and integration software,
complicating direct threat comparisons based solely on nominal range or altitude figures published in
open marketing material. A comprehensive country-by-country accounting of these transfers falls outside
