Islamabad / May 2025 — Pakistan's air defence posture along its eastern approaches continues to be shaped by the HQ-9/P, the export variant of China's long-range surface-to-air missile system supplied by China Precision Machinery Import-Export Corporation (CPMIEC). The system, believed to be operated by the Pakistan Army Air Defence Command, remains a core element of the country's layered air defence architecture, providing coverage against high-value aerial threats including fighter aircraft, cruise missiles, and reportedly some classes of tactical ballistic missiles at extended range.

According to defence sources, the HQ-9/P battery was formally commissioned into Pakistan Army Air Defence service on 27 August 2018 at a ceremony attended by senior Army Air Defence officers, marking what was described at the time as a "new era" in Pakistan's strategic air defence capability. The induction ceremony, held at an undisclosed garrison, reportedly followed a series of trials and technical evaluations conducted through 2017-2018 to assess compatibility with Pakistan's existing air defence command-and-control network.

The HQ-9/P is estimated to have an engagement range of approximately 100-125 km against aerial targets, though some open-source estimates place the maximum intercept range closer to 200 km depending on missile variant and target profile — a figure that remains unconfirmed by official Pakistani sources. The system employs a vertically-launched missile guided by semi-active radar homing in its terminal phase, supported by the HT-233 phased-array engagement radar, which is reportedly capable of simultaneously tracking multiple incoming threats and guiding multiple missiles in flight.

Each HQ-9/P battalion typically comprises a mix of transporter-erector-launcher (TEL) vehicles, the associated engagement and acquisition radars, and a battalion command post, allowing for road-mobile deployment and rapid relocation — a feature considered valuable given the geography of Pakistan's border regions. Analysts note that Pakistan may employ the system primarily to defend high-value fixed assets, including major air bases and command infrastructure, rather than for forward battlefield air defence.

At the August 2018 commissioning ceremony, the then-Corps Commander of Army Air Defence stated that induction of the HQ-9/P "enhances the operational envelope of Pakistan's layered air defence system and provides a credible deterrent against hostile aerial aggression." Pakistani officials at the time also linked the acquisition to the broader modernization drive following the 2017-2018 defence budget cycle, which prioritized air defence upgrades amid regional tensions.

As of this reporting, Islamabad has not issued an updated public statement on the total number of HQ-9/P batteries currently fielded, and estimates among regional defence analysts vary — some placing the figure at a single battalion-sized unit, others suggesting additional batteries may have since been inducted, though this remains unconfirmed. Pakistan's Ministry of Defence has not responded to requests for clarification on current unit strength or deployment locations.

The HQ-9/P's continued prominence in Pakistan's inventory is noteworthy given the broader regional air defence competition, with neighbouring India having inducted the Russian S-400 Triumf system and continuing development of its indigenous Akash and Barak-8 systems. Some defence commentators have drawn comparisons between the HQ-9/P and the Russian S-300PMU-2, on which elements of its design are believed to be based, though Chinese state media has consistently described the system as a domestically-developed product incorporating "indigenous innovations."

Pakistan Army officials have periodically reiterated that the HQ-9/P remains "fully operational and integrated" into the national air defence grid, a characterization repeated in recent statements to visiting foreign military delegations. No independent verification of current operational readiness has been made available.
