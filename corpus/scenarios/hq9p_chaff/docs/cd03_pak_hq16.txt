LY-80 (HQ-16) Medium-Range SAM Update: Pakistan Continues Fielding Amid Regional Modernisation Push
Quwa Defence News Desk
March 2022 · 6 min read

Pakistan's medium-range air defence backbone, the LY-80 — designated in Chinese service as the HQ-16 (红旗-16) — remains in active expansion across Pakistan Army Air Defence Command formations, according to sources familiar with ongoing procurement activity. The system, developed by China National Aero-Technology Import & Export Corporation (CATIC) in cooperation with the China Academy of Launch Vehicle Technology, has been in Pakistani service since deliveries began around 2017, with additional batteries reportedly inducted in the years since.

The LY-80 is a semi-active radar homing (SARH) medium-range SAM, generally credited with an engagement envelope out to roughly 40 km against aircraft targets, though some open-source estimates place the extended-range variant closer to 70 km — a figure that remains unconfirmed publicly. Missiles are vertically launched, typically four rounds per transporter erector launcher (TEL), cued by a associated 3D acquisition and engagement radar believed to be derived from the H-200 family. Analysts differ on whether current Pakistani battalions have received the newer LY-80N naval-derived seeker upgrade or remain on the baseline configuration.

Pakistan's LY-80/HQ-16 batteries are understood to be organised to provide medium-altitude coverage beneath the higher-tier HQ-9/P umbrella now operated by Pakistan Army air defence units, effectively forming a two-layer strategic-to-medium SAM belt along key threat axes. Officials have periodically referenced this layered posture in general terms at defence exhibitions, though specifics on unit basing remain sparse in open sources.

Regional context: India's continued fielding of the Akash and the ongoing induction of the Russian S-400 (NATO: SA-21 'Growler', sometimes referenced as Triumf or Triumph in translated Russian-language material) has reportedly sharpened Pakistani interest in expanding medium-tier SAM density rather than relying solely on the smaller number of strategic HQ-9/P fire units. One regional trade source suggested — without corroboration — that Pakistan may be exploring a follow-on LY-80 batch or a parallel FM-90 short-range acquisition to further densify point defence around key airbases, though no contract has been officially disclosed as of this writing.

Pakistan's air defence modernisation over the past five years has broadly emphasised layering: short-range systems such as the FM-90 and Anza man-portable SAMs, medium-range LY-80/HQ-16 battalions, and the singular long-range HQ-9/P (sometimes rendered HQ-9P in Pakistani service literature, distinct from the CASIC export designation FD-2000) providing the top tier. Analysts caution against conflating the LY-80's medium-range SARH engagement profile with the HQ-9/P's semi-active/track-via-missile long-range intercept role — the two systems, while procured from Chinese industry within a similar timeframe, serve markedly different tactical functions and are not interchangeable in order-of-battle assessments.

No independent confirmation of exact battery counts for either the LY-80 or HQ-9/P fleets has been made available; estimates circulating among regional defence commentators vary considerably, and Quwa was unable to verify unit-level deployment figures with Pakistani officials by press time.
