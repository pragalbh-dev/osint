Pakistan Defence Update — Trade & Industry Briefing
March 2022 Issue

LY-80 (HQ-16) MEDIUM-RANGE SAM CONTINUES ROLLOUT ACROSS AIR DEFENCE COMMANDS

Islamabad — The Pakistan Army's Army Air Defence Command (AADC) has continued to expand its inventory of the LY-80 medium-range surface-to-air missile system, with additional batteries reportedly inducted into service over the past 18 months as part of a broader modernisation of the country's ground-based air defence layer.

The LY-80, marketed by China National Precision Machinery Import & Export Corporation (CPMIEC) and known domestically in China as the HQ-16, was first ordered by Pakistan in the mid-2010s, with deliveries believed to have begun around 2017. Industry sources place the total number of batteries currently fielded somewhere between six and eight, though official figures have not been released and the exact count remains unconfirmed. Each battery is generally understood to comprise a command post, search and engagement radars, and multiple transporter-erector-launchers (TELs), with a typical semi-active radar homing missile round credited with an engagement envelope out to roughly 40 km and altitudes up to around 18-20 km, depending on the source consulted.

Pakistani officials have periodically described the LY-80/HQ-16 as filling the "medium-tier" gap in the national air defence architecture, sitting above shorter-range point-defence systems such as the FM-90 and below the longer-range tier fielded separately by the country. Analysts tracking the program note that the system's road-mobile configuration allows batteries to redeploy relatively quickly between prepared sites, a feature said to be valued given the geography along Pakistan's eastern border.

A defence ministry statement issued last year, cited by regional trade press, described the LY-80 as providing "credible medium-altitude coverage" for critical infrastructure and forward air bases, though it stopped short of specifying which formations had received the system first. Sources close to the AADC suggest that batteries have been distributed to at least two corps-level air defence groups, with further fielding expected to continue through 2022 and into 2023 as CPMIEC deliveries proceed.

The LY-80's radar suite — reportedly derived from the H-200 series associated with the wider HQ-16 family — is said to provide simultaneous tracking of multiple aerial targets, though the precise number handled per engagement radar varies across open-source estimates, with figures ranging from six to twelve depending on the configuration cited. Some reporting has also referenced an export designation of LY-80N for a navalised variant, though there is no indication Pakistan has procured that version.

Maintenance and training support for the system is understood to be provided under a package agreed alongside the original acquisition, with Pakistani technical personnel reportedly having undergone instruction in China during the initial induction phase. A handful of retired officers, speaking to local defence outlets over the past year, have characterised the LY-80 as a "workhorse" of the medium-range layer, credited with providing area coverage that previously relied on older-generation systems nearing the end of their service life.

Regional observers continue to watch for confirmation of additional battery deliveries, with some suggesting the total fleet could grow further before the mid-decade mark, though such projections remain speculative pending further official disclosure.

— Compiled from open industry reporting, March 2022
