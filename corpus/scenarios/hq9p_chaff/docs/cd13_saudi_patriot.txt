Gulf Air Defense Monitor
September 2021 Edition

Saudi Patriot/PAC-3 Batteries Log Continued Intercepts Amid Sustained Houthi Missile and UAV Campaign

Saudi Arabia's Royal Saudi Air Defense Forces (RSADF) reported multiple successful interceptions of Houthi-launched ballistic missiles and one-way attack drones over the past month, according to statements from the Saudi-led coalition and open-source flight tracking of debris fields near Riyadh, Jazan, and Khamis Mushait.

The coalition spokesperson confirmed on 3 September that a ballistic missile targeting an unspecified civilian area in southern Saudi Arabia was intercepted by a Patriot PAC-2/GEM-T battery stationed near Khamis Mushait. Separately, on 11 September, a PAC-3 MSE interceptor reportedly engaged an incoming target believed to be a Houthi Badr-1 or similar solid-fuel short-range ballistic missile; the exact variant remains unconfirmed pending debris analysis. Coalition officials estimated the missile's warhead at approximately 500 kg, though independent analysts have suggested figures ranging from 300 to 550 kg based on fragment sizing.

Saudi Arabia currently fields an estimated 88 Patriot fire units across various configurations (PAC-2, PAC-2 GEM-T, and PAC-3/PAC-3 MSE), procured through multiple Foreign Military Sales cases dating back to the 1990s. Riyadh has reportedly sought to accelerate delivery of additional PAC-3 MSE interceptor stocks given the elevated expenditure rate against Houthi drone swarms, which analysts at a regional think tank estimate may exceed 700 interceptors fired since 2015, though the Saudi government has not released an official tally.

A US State Department notification cleared in August continues to move through congressional review, reportedly covering an unspecified number of PAC-3 MSE missiles and associated support equipment, valued in initial reporting at "up to $650 million," a figure some trade sources believe may be revised downward once final contract terms are set.

Houthi military spokesman Yahya Sarea claimed on state-aligned Al-Masirah television that a "Quds-2" cruise missile and a swarm of Samad-3 drones had struck "sensitive" Saudi Aramco facilities in the Eastern Province on 14 September, a claim Saudi authorities did not directly confirm. Satellite imagery reviewed by a commercial provider showed what appeared to be minor scorching near a storage tank at Ras Tanura, though the cause could not be independently verified and Aramco did not respond to requests for comment by press time.

Analysts continue to debate the cost-exchange ratio of using PAC-3 interceptors, each estimated at $3-4 million, against comparatively low-cost Houthi drones, some believed to cost as little as $10,000-$20,000 to produce. This asymmetry has reportedly driven Saudi interest in supplementing Patriot coverage with lower-cost counter-UAS systems, including Skyguard and reported evaluations of directed-energy prototypes, though no firm procurement decision has been announced.

Riyadh has not disclosed the current operational status of its separate THAAD battery, delivered in 2021, though it is believed to remain focused on higher-altitude ballistic threats originating from Yemen's Saada and Hajjah governorates.
