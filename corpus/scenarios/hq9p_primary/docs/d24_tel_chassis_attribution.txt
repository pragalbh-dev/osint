CSIS Missile Threat — Industrial Base Note
"Who builds the HQ-9? Mapping the attributable and the unattributable tiers"
Reference brief, September 2024

Summary. Efforts to map the HQ-9 / HQ-9BE supply chain from open sources run into a sharp, uneven wall:
some tiers are firmly attributable from public records, while others are effectively opaque. This note
draws that line explicitly, because conflating the two is the most common error in secondary reporting on
Chinese long-range SAM production.

Mobility platform (attributable). The single most consistently attributable deep-tier element of the HQ-9
family is its transporter-erector-launcher (TEL) platform. Across successive editions of the system —
including the HQ-9, HQ-9A and the HQ-9B/HQ-9BE export configurations — the TEL rides a heavy 8x8 special
wheeled chassis produced by the Taian (Wanshan) special-vehicle works of the WS-series lineage (variously
rendered TAS5380 / WS2400-series in trade and vehicle-registry literature). The attribution rests on more
than a single claim: the WS-series chassis appears under the HQ-9 TEL in multiple independent open analyses,
in Chinese heavy-vehicle industry catalogues, and in export-brochure imagery, and the chassis family is
itself a well-documented product line supplied across several Chinese missile programs. For the HQ-9/P and
HQ-9BE fielded by Pakistan, there is no open indication of a substitute chassis; the launcher platform is
therefore a confirmed Taian/Wanshan supply relationship — a genuine deep-tier node that IS attributable.

Fire-control radar (NOT attributable). By explicit contrast, the manufacturer of the HT-233 engagement
radar that the system depends on cannot be established from open sources. Institutional logic points to a
CASIC in-house radar house (the 23rd Research Institute / Beijing Institute of Radio Measurement is the most
frequently suggested candidate), but no public record — export annex, end-user certificate summary, or
Chinese-language industry yearbook — names the HT-233 maker, and the common "CPMIEC makes it" claim confuses
the export-marketing agent for the manufacturer. Deeper still, the radar's transmit/receive modules and
MMICs, the seeker, and the datalink are not open-source-attributable to the HQ-9 at all.

The distinction matters for collection. A supply-chain map that marks the TEL-chassis tier "confirmed" and
the radar tier "unknown/collection gap" is doing its job; one that fills the radar tier with the chassis
tier's confidence, or that downgrades the chassis tier to match the radar's opacity, is not. The former is
a supported attribution; the latter is a hypothesis wearing a fact's clothing.
