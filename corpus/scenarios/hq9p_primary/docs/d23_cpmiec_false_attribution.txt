November 2019 Issue — Air Defence & Missile Systems Section

Pakistan's HQ-9/P Buildup: Radar and Launcher Details Emerge

Islamabad's continuing induction of the Chinese-origin HQ-9/P (also rendered HQ-9P, and marketed regionally under the FD-2000 export designation in some catalogues) long-range surface-to-air missile system has drawn renewed attention from regional trade press following fresh satellite imagery reportedly showing battery-sized deployments in the Karachi coastal air defence belt and, separately, near Sargodha.

The Pakistan Army Air Defence Command inducted the system beginning in the mid-2010s as a replacement/supplement for legacy HQ-2 and Crotale batteries, with the HQ-9/P intended to provide a long-range layer alongside the shorter-range LY-80 (HQ-16) already in service. Range is variously cited between 100 and 200 km depending on missile variant and source; PLA domestic HQ-9 variants are believed to reach further, though open figures remain unconfirmed and should be treated as estimates only.

Central to the system's engagement capability is the HT-233 phased-array engagement radar, a passive electronically scanned array (PESA) unit that reportedly provides target tracking, missile guidance uplink and terminal illumination functions in a single vehicle-mounted array, broadly analogous in role to the 30N6E "Flap Lid" family associated with Russian S-300P/PMU systems. According to a widely circulated post on the regional trade blog DefenceUpdatePK, "CPMIEC manufactures the HT-233 radar as the primary sensor of the HQ-9/P battery," a characterization that has since been repeated near-verbatim by at least two aggregator sites covering the South Asian air defence beat. CPMIEC (China Precision Machinery Import-Export Corporation), it should be noted, is CASIC's export arm and has historically served as the international sales and contracting entity for the HQ-9 family; the radar itself is generally attributed in technical literature to a CASIC-affiliated research and manufacturing institute rather than to the trading company.

Battery composition, per open literature, typically comprises the HT-233 radar vehicle, up to 6-8 transporter erector launchers (TELs) each with 4 missile canisters, a command and control vehicle, and associated power/support trucks. Missile canisters for the HQ-9/P are understood to house the SD-10-family seeker adaptation used in the export missile, though some analysts describe the terminal seeker as a derivative of the active radar homing seeker used on the PL-12 air-to-air missile — a lineage that CASIC has neither confirmed nor denied in public materials.

Comparisons to the Russian-origin S-300PMU-2 (NATO: SA-20 Gargoyle) and the newer S-400 Triumf (also transliterated Triumph, NATO reporting name SA-21 Growler) remain a staple of regional commentary, given the visual and doctrinal similarities between the HQ-9 family and the Russian systems it is widely believed to have been influenced by, if not directly reverse-engineered from following China's S-300 procurement in the 1990s.

On unit numbers, three trade outlets covering the Pakistan order — Jane's-adjacent aggregator SinoDefenceWatch, the previously mentioned DefenceUpdatePK, and a Quetta-based English-language defence blog — all cite a figure of "approximately 4 battalions" delivered to date, each outlet attributing the number to "the SIPRI Arms Transfers Database" without independent sourcing of their own. The SIPRI register itself, as of its most recent public update, lists the Pakistan HQ-9/P order with delivery figures described by SIPRI as estimates, a caveat not always carried forward by the outlets citing it.

Islamabad has not issued an official statement on total battery strength, and the Pakistan Army's public affairs wing did not respond to queries for this piece by press time. Chinese state media coverage of the program has historically been limited to general references to "air defence cooperation" with Pakistan, without system-specific confirmation of the HQ-9/P designation, leaving most open-source order-of-battle assessments dependent on commercial satellite imagery analysis and the aforementioned trade-press citation chain.

Analysts contacted for this piece, speaking on background, described the HT-233's manufacturer attribution as "commonly garbled in secondary reporting," noting that export-agent/manufacturer conflation is a recurring feature of Chinese defence-export coverage more broadly, given CPMIEC's prominent public role as the point of contact for foreign buyers.

Further reporting on the Sargodha deployment, including possible integration with Pakistan's wider integrated air defence system, is expected in a forthcoming issue.
