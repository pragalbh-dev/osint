# Designing an OSINT Analyst Workbench: How Real Intelligence Software Works, and What to Steal for Your ADITI 4.0 Demo

## TL;DR
- **Build an "instrument," not a "dashboard," and make provenance the spine of the whole UI.** Every real intelligence and OSINT tool worth copying (Palantir Gotham, Recorded Future, Maltego, i2) organizes around one idea: an analyst navigates entities → sees the evidence behind every claim → judges source reliability separately from information credibility. Your seven surfaces should all hang off a single evidence/provenance model, and your differentiator is honest uncertainty, not AI cleverness.
- **Speak the tradecraft grammar exactly.** Use a two-axis source-grading scheme modeled on the NATO/Admiralty Code (source reliability A–F × information credibility 1–6), keep ICD 203's hard rule that *probability* (likelihood of a fact) and *analytic confidence* (strength of the evidence base) are never conflated, render a visible probability-yardstick legend, and borrow the MIL-STD-2525/APP-6 **solid = confirmed / dashed = suspected** convention — it maps perfectly onto your "confirmed vs. probable holding" status system.
- **Present AI as a disciplined junior analyst that shows receipts and refuses when it can't prove something.** The HCI evidence is blunt: explanations increase trust *even when wrong*, and confidence scores only help when calibrated. So make citations click-to-exact-line, visually separate observed fact from model inference, treat "insufficient evidence to assess" as a first-class rigorous answer, and avoid a bolted-on chatbot. That posture is exactly what will read as credible to IAF evaluators and defuse the "AI fluff" reflex.

## Key Findings

1. **The universal analyst-tool pattern is entity → graph/map/timeline → evidence, on one shared data model.** Palantir Gotham exposes all analysis (Graph, Gaia map, timeline) over a single ontology with "traceable lineage of all changes"; Maltego's entire loop is "drag an entity → run a transform → new connected entities appear," with each node "something real." Recorded Future's "Intelligence Card" bundles an entity's evidence into one view where "each risk rule trigger is based on specific, collected evidence and the sources are made available… for further examination." At your scale (~15–25 entities), you can implement this literally: one entity store, three linked views, one provenance drawer.

2. **Real tools grade source reliability and information credibility on two separate axes — copy this directly.** The Admiralty/NATO Code (STANAG 2511 / AJP-2.1) grades source reliability A–F and information credibility 1–6, deliberately kept independent "to ensure that the reliability of the source does not influence the assessed accuracy of the report." A "1" requires *actual independent corroboration* from a separate collection path. This is the single most replicable tradecraft artifact for your product: a chip like **B2** on every claim.

3. **ICD 203 forbids conflating likelihood and confidence — your UI must physically separate them.** ICD 203 mandates a seven-term probability ladder (almost no chance / very unlikely / unlikely / roughly even chance / likely / very likely / almost certain, mapped to % bands) and *separately* an analytic-confidence rating (high/moderate/low based on quantity/quality of sources). It "explicitly prohibits combining a confidence level and a likelihood term in the same sentence." The UK PHIA Probability Yardstick does the same with deliberate gaps between bands (so analysts don't argue "unlikely" vs "very unlikely"). Implication: two distinct UI controls/legends, never one blended "confidence: 73%" number.

4. **The MIL-STD-2525/APP-6 dashed-frame convention is a gift for your status system.** In NATO Joint Military Symbology, "present status is indicated by a solid line and planned [or anticipated/suspected] status by a dashed line," and the frame encodes affiliation by *both* shape and color redundantly so it survives a monochrome/night display. This is a battle-tested, accessibility-safe encoding you can adopt: **solid border = confirmed holding, dashed border = suspected/claimed**, with shape or icon (not just color) carrying entity type. Critical caveat from practitioners: the four affiliation colors (cyan friend / red hostile / yellow unknown / green neutral) are "reserved" — do not reuse red for status or severity.

5. **Oryx is the canonical "every claim has a receipt" model.** Oryx documents only "visually confirmed" losses requiring geolocated photo/video evidence, explicitly accepting systematic *undercounting* as the price of rigor ("the amount of equipment destroyed is significantly higher than recorded here"). This is precisely the ethic your product should dramatize: a confirmed count that is defensible beats an inflated count that isn't. It also models honest status categories (destroyed/damaged/abandoned/captured) and anti-double-counting rules.

6. **Practitioners tolerate AI as an augmentation that shows its work — and are actively hostile to chatbots-on-top and "AI fluff."** The r/OSINT community circulates posts titled "You're Not an OSINT Analyst, You're an AI Prompt Monkey" and "The Slow Collapse of Critical Thinking in OSINT due to AI." The consistent practitioner line is "augment, not replace": AI is credible when it does data collection, translation, entity extraction, and triage, and insulting when it claims to do the *judgment*. The CIA's first-ever CTO, Nand Mulchandani, told the Billington Cybersecurity Summit (Sept 2023) that generative AI is "what I call the 'crazy drunk friend'… AI can give you something so far outside of your range, that it really then opens up the vista," but warned "in areas where it requires precision, we're going to be incredibly challenged."

7. **The HCI literature is a red flag for naive "explainability."** Placebic (content-free) explanations raise trust as much as real ones (Eiband et al., CHI 2019); explanations increase acceptance of AI whether right or wrong (Bansal et al., CHI 2021); and confidence scores only calibrate trust when the confidence is itself well-calibrated (Zhang, Liao & Bellamy, FAT* 2020) — miscalibrated confidence is actively "hazardous." The intervention with the best evidence for *reducing* over-reliance is **cognitive forcing functions** (Buçinca & Gajos, CSCW 2021), which make the analyst engage before seeing the AI's answer. Design consequence: don't rely on "show the reasoning" to earn trust; force the analyst to touch the evidence.

8. **Government AI-assessment failures are now concrete and citable — build the UI to prevent exactly this failure mode.** The White House "MAHA" report, released May 22, 2025, contained fabricated citations: of 522 footnotes at least 37 appeared multiple times and some URLs included "oaicite," with NOTUS finding 7 nonexistent studies — the Washington Post (May 29, 2025) called this "a definitive sign that the research was collected using artificial intelligence." Deloitte Australia refunded the final installment, over A$97,000 (~US$63,000), of a ~A$440,000 Department of Employment and Workplace Relations contract (CFO Dive, Oct 21, 2025) after its 237-page Azure-OpenAI-assisted report included a fabricated Federal Court quote and nonexistent references. And in *Mata v. Avianca* (678 F.Supp.3d 443), Judge P. Kevin Castel (SDNY) imposed a $5,000 Rule 11 sanction on June 22, 2023, on the attorneys who filed six ChatGPT-hallucinated cases, calling one fake opinion's analysis "gibberish." All three share one failure mode: fluent, plausible, fabricated citations that passed human review. Your "click citation → exact source line" and "insufficient evidence" behaviors are the direct antidote.

## Details

### 1. How real analyst tools are designed

**Palantir Gotham / Foundry / Blueprint.** Gotham is "built like an internet search engine, with a home screen displaying prominently a search bar." Analysis happens in a unified Workspace across integrated apps: **Graph** (a shared canvas where users "view aggregated property statistics… organise, style, and annotate graphs," collaboratively, backed by the full data foundation and federated systems) and **Gaia** (map: geotag objects, radius/polygon/temporal searches, heatmaps). The whole thing sits on a *dynamic ontology* (people/orgs/assets as objects) with "traceable lineage of all changes." Key transferable ideas at small scale: (a) search bar as the front door; (b) the same entity appears in graph, map and timeline; (c) provenance/lineage is a platform property, not a feature. Palantir also open-sourced **Blueprint.js**, a React toolkit "optimized for building complex, data-dense web interfaces for desktop applications… not a mobile-first UI toolkit" — built for exactly your class of app, and it explicitly relies on "both strong contrast and iconography" (not color alone) for status. For a React/Vite SPA on a 2.5-day budget, Blueprint gives you dark-theme dense tables, tags, tree controls, and toasts out of the box.

**Recorded Future.** The "Intelligence Card" is the model unit: a single-view summary per entity with an overall 0–99 risk score "determined by which rules are currently triggered," where every rule exposes "one or more reporting sources and links back to documents published by these sources." Its browser extension gives "full transparency into the reasoning behind our risk scores." Transferable: a per-entity card with a headline assessment that *decomposes into individually sourced sub-claims* you can click through.

**Maltego.** The graph is the workspace. Entities live in a palette; you drag one onto the canvas and run "transforms" (automated queries) that return connected entities; results "act as a new lead or linking evidence" you pivot from. Bellingcat's own toolkit notes Maltego's **Block Layout** groups results "by importance or similarity" so analysts "prioritize entities based on their connection strength to the main subject." Transferable: pivoting and layout-as-prioritization.

**i2 Analyst's Notebook** remains the reference link-chart tool and is used to "map relationships between people, events, and data" supporting structured hypothesis testing; the PARC/Pherson **ACH tool** is the reference structured-technique implementation (matrix of hypotheses × evidence).

**Satellite/geospatial analytics UIs** (Planet, Sentinel Hub/EO Browser, ArcGIS, ISRO's Bhuvan) share a layers + time-slider + AOI (area-of-interest) draw model. For your map surface, the replicable pieces are a layer toggle, a time/freshness control, and draw-a-polygon selection.

### 2. Tradecraft standards that should shape the UI

- **Admiralty/NATO source grading (A–F × 1–6).** Render as a compact two-part chip (e.g., **B2**) on every source and every claim. Provide a legend/tooltip with the definitions. Reliability is a *track record* judgment (about the source); credibility is a *this-item* judgment (about the specific report, driven by corroboration). Keep the axes visually separate. Note the honest "cannot be judged" grades (F and 6) — these are the tradecraft ancestors of your "insufficient evidence" state.
- **ICD 203 + ICD 206.** ICD 206 governs sourcing (identify sources, describe access, currency, bias, denial-and-deception). ICD 203 governs the analytic product: separate likelihood from confidence; distinguish underlying information from assumptions and judgments; assess alternative hypotheses. Your "Ask" answers should structurally mirror this: a claim, its likelihood term, its confidence rating (shown separately), its sources, and a one-line "what would change this."
- **Probability yardsticks.** Show the ODNI seven-term ladder *or* the PHIA yardstick as a persistent legend. PHIA deliberately leaves gaps between bands; ODNI maps terms to % ranges (e.g., "likely" ≈ 55–80%). Note that these ranges are imperfectly understood: a 924-participant study (Collins et al., "Verbal probabilities: Very likely to be somewhat more confusing than numbers," PLOS One, 2019) found "almost perfect correspondence… for Roughly Even Chance but poorer correspondence for Unlikely and Likely categories," and observed that "people interpret verbal expressions of probabilities (e.g. 'very likely') in different ways, yet words are commonly preferred to numbers." Recommendation: use **words as primary, % band as secondary/tooltip** — because verbal terms alone are interpreted inconsistently, but numeric-only feels falsely precise, so showing both (with the legend) hedges the known failure of each.
- **Analytic confidence ≠ probability.** This is the single most-cited tradecraft error. Encode them on different visual channels (see §4).
- **Structured Analytic Techniques.** ACH (hypotheses as columns, evidence as rows; you seek the hypothesis with the *least inconsistent* evidence, not the most supporting) is directly renderable as a matrix and pairs naturally with your "Ask" view showing alternatives. Key Assumptions Check and Indicators & Warning map to your alerts/tripwires surface (an I&W indicator is literally a tripwire). Dissent in US NIEs is expressed as footnoted alternative views — a precedent for a first-class "alternative view" affordance rather than burying disagreement.
- **India/IAF specifics.** Public material is thin. The IAF's AI-OSINT work runs under the **UDAAN** framework and an "AI Centre of Excellence"; the ADITI 4.0 problem statement (IAF) is literally "AI Based OSINT Analysis and monitoring system, guidance and social media stacks." There is no public India-specific probability lexicon, so adopting the NATO/ODNI/PHIA conventions (and saying so) is defensible and reads as rigor.

### 3. "AI fluff" — what practitioners actually tolerate

**What reads as insulting/untrustworthy:** a chatbot bolted onto a tool; claims that AI does the *analysis/judgment*; "predictive" language implying the model forecasts events; hidden reasoning; confidence numbers with false precision; anthropomorphized "the AI thinks…" framing. The r/OSINT discourse is openly derisive of prompt-monkey workflows and "critical thinking collapse."

**What reads as credible:** framing AI as doing collection, translation, entity/relationship extraction, deduplication, and triage — with the human owning judgment. Language practitioners respect: "augment," "surfacing candidates for review," "flagged for analyst confirmation," "draft," "extracted from source." The CIA's own framing is instructive: gen-AI "won't do the thinking for our analysts, but it will help draft key judgments, edit for clarity and compare drafts against tradecraft standards."

**The automation-bias evidence.** Automation bias — "the tendency to use automated cues as a heuristic replacement for vigilant information seeking" — is well documented in aviation and medicine and now in AI decision support. In national-security contexts specifically, over-reliance is nonlinear in expertise (novices under- or over-trust; the relationship "bends"). Distrust after a single visible failure is durable. Implication: the UI must make verification *cheaper than blind acceptance*, and must never hide when it's guessing.

**The explainability trap (well-evidenced, and it contradicts a naive design instinct):**
- Placebic explanations invoke "perceived levels of trust similar to real explanations" (Eiband, Buschek, Kremer & Hussmann, CHI 2019) — so a nice-looking rationale is not evidence of a good rationale; the authors warn this "placebo effect seems potentially worrisome if used to 'deceive' users… comparable to 'dark' UX patterns."
- Explanations increased acceptance of the AI "whether or not it was correct" and failed to produce complementary team gains (Bansal et al., CHI 2021).
- Confidence scores help calibrate trust "but trust calibration alone is not sufficient to improve AI-assisted decision making" (Zhang, Liao & Bellamy, FAT* 2020); and miscalibrated confidence is "hazardous."
- **Cognitive forcing functions** "significantly reduced overreliance on AI compared to… presenting explanations" (Buçinca, Malaya & Gajos, CSCW 2021), but users *disliked* them and high-Need-for-Cognition users benefited most.

**Documented failures to cite as your motivating "why."** MAHA report fabricated citations with "oaicite" markers (May 2025); Deloitte Australia refund over fabricated references and a made-up judicial quote (Oct 2025); *Mata v. Avianca* Rule 11 sanctions for hallucinated cases (2023). The design lesson is identical across all three: fabricated-but-fluent citations pass human review unless the tool makes the *exact source* one click away.

### 4. Visual / interaction patterns to steal

**(a) Encoding uncertainty without a Christmas tree.** The academic answer is **Value-Suppressing Uncertainty Palettes** (Correll, Moritz & Heer, CHI 2018): "allocate larger ranges of a visual channel to data when uncertainty is low, and smaller ranges when uncertainty is high," so uncertain values "retreat into a confidence fog" and all read as similar grey — this "encourages more cautious decision-making when uncertainty is high." Combine with multi-channel redundant encoding so no single channel is overloaded and it survives a colorblind viewer / low-contrast projector:
   - **Categorical status** (confirmed/suspected/refuted) → border style (solid/dashed/dotted) + icon, per MIL-STD-2525.
   - **Continuous freshness/decay** → opacity or a "confidence fog" desaturation (VSUP-style), plus an explicit timestamp.
   - **Tertiary source-tier** → a small corner chip (the A–F grade), not a third color ramp.
   This gives you three independent channels (shape/border, value/opacity, text chip) instead of three competing hues. Hullman & Kay's broader corpus (quantile dotplots, HOPs) is overkill for pins but useful if you show any distribution.

**(b) Provenance and citation UI.** The best-practice pattern (from Perplexity/ChatGPT/Elicit/Consensus/Scite/NotebookLM teardowns): inline numbered chip or superscript at the *claim*, hover/tap preview card (source title, domain, snippet), and a full source-list side rail. "When sources are dumped only at the end… most do not bother" to verify; inline citations "bring the source to the claim, exactly where doubt arises." What makes a citation feel trustworthy vs decorative: it points to the *specific passage* not a homepage ("citation overreach" is when it links to a broadly relevant page but not the claim); chips are real focusable elements; and "faking citation chips for unsupported content is a trust-killer." Behavioral reality: most users *read the answer without clicking* — so the citation must communicate credibility at a glance (named source + date + grade), and clicking should jump to the exact highlighted line. Legal-research tools (Westlaw/Lexis/CoCounsel/Harvey) and clinical decision support enforce "click to exact line" because a fabricated cite is a fireable offense — mirror that standard.

**(c) Distinguishing observed fact from model inference.** This is under-served by existing products and is a genuine differentiator. Precedents to lean on: C2PA/Content Credentials' distinction between "verifiable history" and "truthful content"; ICD 203's mandate to "clearly distinguish underlying intelligence information from assumptions and judgments." Concretely: render observed facts (with citations) in one type treatment, and model-generated inference/synthesis in a visually distinct treatment (e.g., a tinted "inference" band, a different left-border, or an explicit "model inference — not directly sourced" label). Never let a synthesized sentence look identical to a sourced one.

**(d) Graph explorer at 15–25 nodes.** This is small — you will not have a hairball, so optimize for *readability and emphasis*, not scale. Library choice: **Cytoscape.js** is "the richest all-in-one graph toolkit… when algorithms and layouts are part of the product" (it has built-in centrality/pathfinding, which you need to compute and highlight an articulation point/chokepoint), styled with CSS-like stylesheets. Sigma.js/Graphology is for large WebGL graphs (not your case); vis-network is the fastest path to an editable physics diagram. Recommendation: **Cytoscape.js** with a deterministic layout (cose or a manual/preset layout) so the graph looks identical every demo. Encode edge *type* by line style/label and edge *status* (confirmed/suspected) by solid/dashed — same convention as pins, so the two views feel unified. Emphasize the chokepoint by node size (betweenness centrality) + a halo/ring, and dim non-adjacent nodes on selection.

**(e) Map + graph linked views.** Brushing/linking (select in one view → highlight in the other) is the proven pattern. For a live demo on one desktop screen, **switchable with a persistent selection** beats side-by-side (side-by-side halves your legibility at compressed bitrate). Recommendation: a primary view with a toggle, where selecting an entity anywhere (map pin, graph node, Ask citation, review card) highlights it everywhere and opens the same provenance drawer. That single "select once, highlighted everywhere" behavior is the most impressive thing you can show on a call.

**(f) Human-in-the-loop review queue.** Entity-resolution merge-review is the closest analog (Senzing/Quantexa/Tamr/OpenRefine): the review card shows the two candidate records *side by side with matching fields aligned and the evidence for the match*, plus one-tap Merge / Not-a-match / Defer. Senzing's "entity-centric" model even "revises past match assertions… as new evidence appears." What makes a review card fast and safe: (1) the proposed action is explicit and reversible; (2) the *reason for the suggestion* is shown ("matched on: designation + geolocation"); (3) the source evidence is one click away; (4) keyboard-driven accept/reject. Content-moderation and medical second-read queues add: show the confidence, batch similar items, and log who decided what (audit trail).

**(g) "Honest refusal" / insufficient-evidence UI.** Make it read as rigor: don't show an empty state or an error. Show *what was searched, what was found, what's missing, and what would resolve it* — mirroring ACH's "identify milestones for future observation" and the Admiralty "6 — truth cannot be judged." A refusal card should list: the question, the sources checked, the specific corroboration gap ("1 uncorroborated source, grade C3 — needs a second independent source"), and a "collect this to resolve" line. This is directly modeled on how NIEs and PHIA products caveat, and it's your strongest anti-hallucination demo beat.

**(h) Stale/decaying data.** Show "true as of \<date\>" plus a decay indicator (the VSUP fog again, or an explicit "last confirmed 14 months ago — may be outdated" chip). Oryx's discipline (a claim is only as good as its last visual confirmation) is the mental model. For an air-defense ORBAT, freshness is analytically critical (a SAM battery moves), so surface last-confirmed date prominently on every pin and card.

### 5. ORBAT and military-visual conventions

- **MIL-STD-2525/APP-6 symbol anatomy:** frame (affiliation shape + color + status) + fill + central icon + modifiers (echelon ticks above, mobility below, text fields). Affiliation is redundantly shape+color; **status is solid (present) vs dashed (planned/anticipated/suspected)**. This is your confirmed-vs-suspected encoding, ready-made and doctrinally literate — using it signals to IAF evaluators that you speak their visual language.
- **Reserved colors:** cyan=friend, red=hostile, yellow=unknown, green=neutral. Don't repurpose these for confidence/severity.
- **milsymbol.js** is a mature JS renderer (SVG output, pairs with Leaflet/MapLibre) if you want authentic symbols; but for ~15–25 pins, simplified custom markers using the solid/dashed + icon convention are lighter and fully sufficient.
- **Confirmed vs claimed in commercial OSINT:** Oryx = visual confirmation required, conservative undercount, explicit status categories, photo receipt per entry. Janes and IISS Military Balance present holdings with sourcing caveats; the Oryx "every entry links to its photo/video" model is the most demo-friendly and maps onto "every claim has a receipt."

### 6. Adjacent-domain precedents

- **C2PA / Content Credentials** is directly relevant to your "recycled 2019 photo" beat. It records "who made it, when, and what tools were used" in a tamper-evident manifest, surfaced in-UI as an icon/badge/info-panel (as on LinkedIn, or contentcredentials.org/verify). Crucially, C2PA proves *history, not truth*: "a camera can sign a photo of a screen displaying a deepfake." And ordinary uploads (WhatsApp, Facebook) strip the manifest. Design lesson: show a provenance badge on any image, expandable to a manifest/history panel; but frame it as "provenance chain," not "authenticity guarantee," and explicitly handle "no credentials present" (the common case) without implying fakery.
- **Reverse-image / image provenance** (TinEye, Google Lens/Yandex): the "earliest known appearance" date is the key datum for catching recycled imagery — surface "first seen: 2019" on the media card.
- **Fact-checking/provenance** (InVID-WeVerify, Hoaxy, Google Fact Check Explorer, ClaimReview): ClaimReview's structured "claim → rating → source" triple is a clean template for your claim cards.
- **AML/e-discovery** (Chainalysis Reactor, Quantexa, Relativity, Everlaw): mature at *audit trails, entity resolution review, and defensible chains of evidence* — the review-queue and provenance-drawer patterns above come largely from this lineage.

### 7. Aesthetic direction: "instrument," not "dashboard"

Shared traits of Bloomberg Terminal, trading terminals, code editors, mission-control and medical-monitoring displays: high information density; small, consistent monospaced/tabular numerals; dark or low-luminance backgrounds; restrained accent-color palette used *semantically* (color means something, it's not decoration); minimal chrome; keyboard-first navigation; and stable layouts that don't animate gratuitously. "Instrument" reads as: every pixel is information, color is a signal not a mood, and the operator is trusted with density. "Dashboard" reads as: big rounded cards, hero numbers, lots of whitespace, decorative gradients. For gov/defense credibility, borrow from **Blueprint.js** (built for this), and reference **GOV.UK Design System / USWDS** for accessible, plain, high-contrast components. Concretely: dark theme, one accent for "confirmed," desaturated tones for "suspected/stale," tabular numerals for counts/dates, dense tables with hover rows, and a monospace treatment for IDs/grades.

### 8. Live-demo craft

- **Legibility at compressed bitrate:** larger base font than you think, high contrast, avoid thin 1px light-grey lines and subtle hue differences (screen-share codecs destroy them). Test your palette on a projector/second monitor.
- **Avoid hover-dependent reveals:** anything critical (citations, provenance, grades) must be visible or click-revealed, not hover-only — on a shared screen the audience can't see your cursor hover, and codec smearing kills tooltips. Provide a click/pin behavior for the provenance drawer.
- **Animation that survives screen-share:** prefer instant state changes or short (<200ms) transitions; avoid continuous physics/force animation during the talk (freeze the graph layout). A deterministic, pre-laid-out graph looks the same every run.
- **How gov/defense demos are judged:** against the tradecraft standards (does it separate probability from confidence? does it source claims? does it refuse honestly?), against operational realism, and against whether a *single analyst* can actually drive it. Scripting a linear "golden path" (open entity → see graph+map → ask a question → get cited answer → hit an insufficient-evidence wall → resolve via review queue → watch a tripwire fire) demonstrates all seven surfaces in one narrative.

## Notes on other useful things found

- **The ADITI 4.0 problem statement is confirmed and specific:** IAF, "AI Based OSINT Analysis and monitoring system, guidance and social media stacks," under iDEX; the IAF's broader AI-OSINT effort sits under the UDAAN framework and an AI Centre of Excellence. Grounding your demo narrative in "monitoring + guidance + social media stacks" language will resonate.
- **"Absence of evidence is evidence" (ACH discipline):** treat a missing expected indicator as a data point. A sophisticated touch: an entity card that notes "expected but not observed: no associated radar emitter" as a confidence-lowering factor.
- **Corroboration is the atomic trust operation.** Across Admiralty (a "1" needs independent confirmation), Perplexity (looks for cross-domain consensus), and Oryx (independent geolocation), the recurring primitive is: *count independent sources*. Make "N independent sources" a visible, computed field on every claim — it's cheap to implement and deeply legible to intelligence people.
- **milsymbol.js SVG performance ceiling** (~1,500 symbols) is irrelevant at your scale — noted only so you don't over-engineer.
- **Cognitive-forcing as a feature, not a bug:** consider a deliberate "review the evidence before revealing the AI's assessment" step in the Ask view for high-stakes queries. It's research-backed against over-reliance (Buçinca & Gajos, CSCW 2021) and demos as rigor — though keep it optional/fast given users dislike friction.

## Recommendations (prioritized for ~2.5 days of design + a live demo)

**Tier 1 — do these first; they define the product's soul:**
1. **Make the provenance drawer the center of gravity.** One drawer, invoked by selecting any entity/claim/pin/node, showing: the claim, its Admiralty-style grade (e.g., B2), N independent sources as clickable chips, each chip opening the exact source line/photo, plus "first seen" date for media. If you build one thing well, build this.
2. **Separate probability from confidence, visibly.** Two distinct controls/legends. Words primary (ODNI/PHIA ladder), % band secondary. Never a single blended number. Put a small persistent yardstick legend on screen — it's a one-glance signal you know the tradecraft.
3. **Adopt solid = confirmed / dashed = suspected everywhere** (pins, graph edges, cards), with shape/icon carrying type and opacity carrying freshness. This is your coherent, accessibility-safe, doctrinally literate visual system. Reserve red for hostile affiliation only.
4. **Build the "insufficient evidence to assess" card as a hero moment,** not an error state: question → sources checked → specific corroboration gap → what would resolve it. This is your single most differentiating anti-hallucination beat.

**Tier 2 — high impact, moderate effort:**
5. **Cited multi-hop Ask answers that structurally separate observed fact (sourced) from model inference (visually distinct band/label).** Every sentence either has a citation or is explicitly marked inference. Click any citation → jump to highlighted source line.
6. **Linked selection across views** ("select once, highlighted everywhere") with a switchable map/graph rather than side-by-side. This is the highest wow-per-effort interaction on a call.
7. **Review-queue cards** in the entity-resolution idiom: side-by-side candidates, matching fields aligned, "matched on: …" reason, one-tap reversible Merge/Reject/Defer, evidence one click away, keyboard-driven.

**Tier 3 — polish that signals maturity:**
8. **Credibility-weights settings panel** that visibly recomputes claim grades when a source's reliability is adjusted — demonstrates the two-axis model is real, not cosmetic.
9. **Tripwires framed as Indicators & Warning:** each alert names the indicator it watches and links to the evidence that tripped it.
10. **Instrument aesthetic via Blueprint.js:** dark, dense, tabular numerals, semantic color only, keyboard nav; test legibility on a projector; freeze graph layouts; make everything critical click- not hover-revealed.

**Benchmarks that would change these recommendations:** if the demo audience turns out to be non-technical decision-makers rather than analysts, dial density down and lead with the map + the plain-language "insufficient evidence" narrative. If you get real image data with C2PA credentials, promote the provenance-badge/manifest panel to Tier 1. If graph size grows past ~150 nodes in a later phase, switch from Cytoscape.js SVG to Sigma.js/WebGL. If you cannot get click-to-exact-line working, do *not* ship fake-looking citation chips — a visible "source list" without false precision is more trustworthy than decorative inline chips.

- **A genuine tension with the current direction:** the instinct to "show the AI's reasoning to build trust" is partly contradicted by the research — explanations raise trust even when wrong (placebic-explanation effect). The safer design is to show *evidence and sources* (which are verifiable) rather than *reasoning narratives* (which are persuasive whether or not they're sound), and to consider a cognitive-forcing step for high-stakes answers.
